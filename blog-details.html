10 Essential Kayaking Tips for First-Time Paddlers

Marcus Webb — Head River Guide
May 1, 2026 — 8 min read
Home › Blog › 10 Kayaking Tips

Getting on the water for the first time is one of life's great thrills. The sound of the current, the splash of a paddle, the scent of forest air — kayaking connects us to nature in a way few activities can match. But before you launch: a little preparation goes a long way.

After 15 years guiding hundreds of first-timers on Pacific Northwest rivers, I've distilled the most important lessons into 10 tips every beginner should know.

1. Choose the Right Kayak for Your Trip

Not all kayaks are created equal. Sit-on-top kayaks are ideal for beginners — they're stable, self-draining and you can easily remount if you flip. Sit-inside kayaks offer better protection on cold water but require a wet exit technique lesson. At RiverRun, we always recommend a short test float in calm water before tackling moving sections.

"The best kayak for a beginner is a wide, stable hull in flat water — save the performance boats for after you've logged your first 20 hours on the water."

2. Master the Forward Stroke First

Most beginners don't know that power in kayaking comes from your torso, not your arms. Rotate your entire upper body as you plant each blade deep in the water near your feet — not beside your hips. This will instantly make you faster, more efficient and dramatically reduce arm fatigue.

3. Always Wear Your PFD

A personal flotation device (PFD) that stays on a hook in your boat is completely useless if you flip. Wear it. Every. Single. Time. We don't allow anyone on RiverRun trips without a properly fitted, USCG-approved PFD — and for very good reason.

4. Learn to Read River Current

Rivers have "features" — eddies, pools, rapids and hazards. An eddy (the calm water downstream of a rock or bend) is your friend — it's where you rest and regroup. Current reads differently depending on depth and width. Start on Class I water to learn how a river moves before tackling anything faster.

Taking a guided trip first is often the best way to accelerate your learning. Certified instructors can provide real-time feedback that prevents bad habits from forming early on.
